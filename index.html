What this data is really telling you about our buyer

Every chart above is a window into one person: the preparedness-minded woman, 65+, who buys TerraStrike not for herself but for the people she's terrified of losing — a child, a grandchild, anyone she pictures trapped in a car that won't open. (The 30–50 "protective mom" is who we want; the data shows who actually shows up.) She isn't buying a glass breaker. She's buying the feeling of being the one who was ready.

  • Segments = identity, not size. "Who they protect" (solo → x2, family → x8) is the single most important answer in the quiz. It's an identity label, not a quantity: she's claiming a role — protector of the family. Mirror that role back at checkout and in email; don't just show a bigger bundle.
  • Where the funnel bleeds = a belief you haven't fixed yet. The #1 barrier for this product is confusion — "what even is this keychain?" A big early drop-off isn't a UX bug; it's people who never felt the stakes (trapped in water, fire, electronic / Tesla doors) before being asked to decide. Raise the emotion first, then ask.
  • Top states aren't geography — they're which fear to lead with. Flood / water states and high-accident states want different opening images. Localize the danger: "risk by state" earns the click because it feels like it's about her road.
  • The answer distribution is your amygdala heat-map. The answers chosen most are the angles that fire hardest on cold traffic. Promote those in ads — the depth of the emotion you tap is the number-one predictor of how this funnel converts.

Rule of thumb: read the biggest drop-off bar as a question — what does she need to believe here that she doesn't yet? — and write the next ad to that, not to the product.
